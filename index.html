The Shadow Fleet in 2025: How 600+ Sanctioned Tankers Move Oil Undetected

✍ Content Agent • Draft generated Mar 12, 2026 Shadow FleetSanctions ComplianceAIS Intelligence

The shadow fleet is no longer a fringe phenomenon. More than 600 oil tankers now operate outside Western insurance, flagging, and oversight systems — and together they move an estimated $140 billion in sanctioned crude every year. For compliance teams, port authorities, and commodity traders, understanding how these vessels operate isn’t optional. It’s baseline due diligence.

What Is the Shadow Fleet?

The shadow fleet — also known as the dark fleet — refers to tankers that have severed ties with Western maritime infrastructure: P&I insurance, flag states with transparency requirements, and Western vetting systems. Most emerged after 2022 Russia sanctions created demand for vessels willing to move sanctioned crude without paper trails that reach Western counterparties.

Three Tactics Shadow Fleet Vessels Use to Evade Detection

1
AIS Spoofing
Vessels broadcast false position data, making them appear at anchor in a legitimate port while physically transshipping cargo at sea. Windward’s models detect spoofing by cross-referencing satellite AIS with terrestrial feeds and flagging behavioral anomalies that static position data can’t explain.
2
Ship-to-Ship Transfers
Cargo transfers mid-ocean in areas outside surveillance zones — east of Malta, Cape of Good Hope, Strait of Malacca. This breaks the paper trail between sanctioned origin and final destination, making the cargo appear clean to downstream buyers and banks.
3
Flag Hopping
Vessels change flag state mid-voyage to obscure ownership history. In 2024, Windward tracked 84 vessels that changed flags three or more times within 12 months. Most use flags of convenience with minimal transparency requirements.

Why Static Screening Misses This

Most KYV checks rely on point-in-time ownership records and OFAC SDN list matches. Shadow fleet operators exploit this by maintaining clean paper companies above obfuscated beneficial ownership structures, using flags that don’t require transparent registry data, and completing cargo transfers during deliberate AIS dark periods.
